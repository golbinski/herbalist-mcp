# Valerian

Valerian root is the most clinically studied herbal sedative in the Western tradition. Its pungent, earthy smell — often described as dirty socks — belies its effectiveness as a sleep aid and anxiolytic for people who find [[Chamomile]] or [[Lavender]] insufficient.

## Properties

Valerian's mechanism is multifactorial. Key constituents include valerenic acid (modulates GABA-A receptors), isovaleric acid, and iridoid compounds (valepotriates). Unlike [[Lavender]], valerian does not appear to act as a simple GABA agonist — its sedative effect seems to arise from reduced GABA breakdown rather than direct agonism.

- **Sedative** — reduces sleep latency, increases deep sleep stages
- **Anxiolytic** — effective for generalised anxiety, though evidence is less robust than for sleep
- **Antispasmodic** — reduces muscle tension, useful for tension headaches
- **Hypotensive** — mild blood pressure lowering effect

## Medicinal Uses

### Insomnia

Valerian's strongest evidence base is in sleep-onset insomnia. Most clinical trials use 300–600 mg of standardised extract taken 30–60 minutes before bed. Effects accumulate over 2–4 weeks of consistent use — it does not always work as a single-dose remedy.

Pairs well with [[Chamomile]] for mild cases, or with [[Lavender]] for anxiety-driven insomnia.

### Anxiety

Clinical evidence for anxiety is promising but less consistent than for sleep. Works best for somatic anxiety (tension, restlessness, palpitations) rather than cognitive anxiety.

### Muscle Tension and Cramps

The antispasmodic action makes valerian useful for menstrual cramps and tension headaches. [[Ginger]] is a useful partner for cramp formulas.

## Preparation

| Method | Use Case | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| [[Tinctures\|Tincture]] | Sleep, anxiety | 4–6 ml in water, 45 min before bed |
| Capsule (standardised) | Sleep | 300–600 mg standardised to 0.8% valerenic acid |
| [[Teas and Infusions\|Tea]] | Mild sleep aid | Strong decoction of root; taste is an acquired one |

Tincture is generally preferred over tea — the active compounds are better extracted in alcohol than in water.

## Cautions

- **Morning grogginess** at doses above 600 mg — start low
- **Pregnancy and lactation**: avoid (valepotriates have theoretical mutagenic risk in animal studies)
- **CNS depressants**: additive sedation with alcohol, benzodiazepines, and opioids — do not combine without medical supervision
- **Driving**: use caution the morning after a high dose
- Paradoxical stimulation reported in some individuals, particularly children
