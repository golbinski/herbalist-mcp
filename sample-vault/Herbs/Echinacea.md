# Echinacea

Echinacea is the most widely sold herbal medicine in North America and Europe, primarily for immune support and cold prevention. Its reputation as an immune stimulant has been validated by modern research, though the picture is more nuanced than popular marketing suggests.

## Properties

Echinacea's bioactivity comes from a complex mixture of constituents that varies significantly by species and plant part: alkamides (E. purpurea and E. angustifolia roots), polysaccharides (primarily aerial parts), caffeic acid derivatives (echinacoside, cichoric acid), and glycoproteins.

- **Immunomodulatory** — stimulates macrophage activation, NK cell activity, and cytokine production
- **Antiviral** — direct antiviral activity against influenza, rhinovirus, and herpes simplex virus
- **Antibacterial** — inhibits bacterial hyaluronidase (limits tissue penetration)
- **Anti-inflammatory** — alkamides modulate prostaglandin synthesis (paradoxically, it both stimulates immune response and limits excessive inflammation)

## Medicinal Uses

### Cold and Flu Prevention

The evidence for echinacea reducing cold incidence is mixed but overall positive — a 2015 Cochrane review found modest reductions in cold incidence and duration. Most trials suggest it reduces colds by 10–20% and duration by 1–2 days.

[[Elderberry]] is a comparable and complementary herb for antiviral immune support.

### Shortening Cold Duration

Taken at first symptoms (large initial dose, then standard dosing), echinacea appears more effective than prophylactic use. It works best in the first 24–48 hours.

### Wound Healing (Topical)

Topical echinacea preparations reduce inflammation and accelerate wound healing. See [[Salves and Poultices]].

### Recurrent Infections

As an [[Adaptogens|adaptogen]], echinacea may help reduce susceptibility to recurrent upper respiratory infections in people under high stress loads. [[Elderberry]] syrup is a useful partner in this context.

| Method | Use Case | Notes |

| [[Teas and Infusions\|Tea]] | Daily immune support | Root decoction (simmer 20 min) or aerial parts infusion |
| Capsule | Convenience | 300–500 mg standardised extract, 3× daily |
| [[Salves and Poultices\|Topical]] | Wound healing, skin infections | Tincture diluted or infused oil |

### Species and Part Matters

- **E. purpurea aerial parts**: best studied, reliable, widely available
- **E. angustifolia root**: highest alkamide content, stronger immunostimulant
- **E. pallida root**: more antiviral action via echinacoside

## Cautions

- **Autoimmune conditions** (lupus, MS, rheumatoid arthritis): theoretical concern about immune stimulation — use cautiously or avoid
- **Continuous long-term use**: traditional use is pulse-dosing (10 days on, 3–5 days off) though evidence for this recommendation is thin
- **Asteraceae allergy**: cross-reactivity possible (same family as [[Chamomile]])
- **Immunosuppressants**: may reduce efficacy of immunosuppressant drugs
