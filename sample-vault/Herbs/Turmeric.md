# Turmeric

Turmeric is the golden-yellow rhizome of *Curcuma longa*, a close relative of [[Ginger]]. Used for over 4000 years in Ayurvedic and Traditional Chinese medicine, turmeric has become the most studied herb in modern phytochemical research, primarily for its anti-inflammatory curcuminoid compounds.

## Properties

Turmeric's fame rests largely on curcumin, which makes up 2–5% of the dried root. However, turmeric also contains turmerone (volatile oil with neuroprotective and antiparasitic activity), demethoxycurcumin, bisdemethoxycurcumin, and polysaccharides.

- **Anti-inflammatory** — curcumin inhibits NF-κB, COX-2, LOX, and TNF-α at multiple points in the inflammatory cascade; comparable to some NSAIDs in joint pain trials
- **Antioxidant** — both direct radical scavenging and upregulation of endogenous antioxidant enzymes (Nrf2 pathway)
- **Cholagogue** — stimulates bile production, supporting fat digestion and liver function
- **Neuroprotective** — crosses the blood-brain barrier; promotes BDNF; studied for Alzheimer's prevention
- **Antimicrobial** — active against H. pylori and several fungi

## Medicinal Uses

### Joint Pain and Osteoarthritis

The best-evidenced use. Multiple RCTs show curcumin (1000–1500 mg/day) reduces pain and improves function in knee osteoarthritis comparably to 800 mg ibuprofen in some trials, with superior gastrointestinal tolerability. Works synergistically with [[Ginger]] in anti-inflammatory formulas.

### Inflammatory Bowel Disease

Curcumin as an adjunct to conventional treatment shows promise in maintaining remission in ulcerative colitis. [[Ginger]] complements this for motility and nausea.

### Liver Support

Turmeric's cholagogue action and antioxidant properties support liver detoxification. Traditional use for fatty liver, jaundice, and post-illness recovery.

### Metabolic Syndrome

Curcumin improves insulin sensitivity, reduces fasting blood glucose, and has modest lipid-lowering effects in metabolic syndrome.

## Bioavailability Challenge

Curcumin is poorly absorbed from the gut — oral bioavailability is very low. Key strategies to improve absorption:

| Strategy | Mechanism | Example |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| Black pepper (piperine) | Inhibits CYP3A4 and P-glycoprotein | Traditional Ayurvedic combination |
| Fat co-ingestion | Curcumin is fat-soluble | Take with a meal containing fat |
| Phytosome (Meriva) | Phospholipid complex | Commercial supplement |
| Nanoparticle formulations | Enhanced solubility | Various commercial products |

Traditional "golden milk" (turmeric + fat + black pepper) is an elegant folk solution to this bioavailability problem.

## Preparation

| Method | Use Case | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| Golden milk | Daily anti-inflammatory, joints | Turmeric + black pepper + fat in warm milk |
| [[Teas and Infusions\|Tea/decoction]] | Digestion, liver | Simmer fresh or dried root 10–15 min |
| [[Tinctures\|Tincture]] | Liver, digestion | 2–4 ml, 3× daily; less suited for joint conditions due to low bioavailability |
| Capsule | Joint pain, IBD | 500–750 mg curcumin + piperine, 2–3× daily with food |
| [[Salves and Poultices\|Topical poultice]] | Skin inflammation, bruising | Paste of fresh root; stains skin yellow |

## Cautions

- **Gallstones and bile duct obstruction**: cholagogue effect may trigger biliary colic
- **Blood thinners** (warfarin, aspirin, clopidogrel): high-dose curcumin has antiplatelet activity; monitor
- **Iron absorption**: high doses may reduce iron absorption — separate from iron supplements
- **Surgery**: stop high-dose supplementation 2 weeks before surgery (bleeding risk)
- **Pregnancy**: food amounts are safe; supplemental doses should be avoided
