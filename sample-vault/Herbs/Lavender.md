# Lavender

Lavender is perhaps the most universally recognised aromatic herb, valued for millennia across Mediterranean and Middle Eastern traditions. Beyond its fragrance, it carries a well-documented pharmacological profile targeting anxiety, sleep, pain, and skin repair.

## Properties

The primary active constituents are linalool and linalyl acetate, which act on GABA-A receptors in ways similar to benzodiazepines — without the dependency risk. Additional constituents include camphor, 1,8-cineole, and flavonoids.

- **Anxiolytic** — clinical evidence supports its use for generalised anxiety; oral lavender oil (Silexan) is licensed in Germany for this indication
- **Sedative** — reduces sleep latency and improves sleep quality
- **Analgesic** — topical and inhaled lavender reduces pain perception
- **Antimicrobial** — effective against a range of gram-positive bacteria and fungi
- **Anti-inflammatory** — reduces inflammatory cytokine expression

## Medicinal Uses

### Anxiety and Mood

Lavender's anxiolytic effect is well supported by RCTs. Inhaled (aromatherapy) or oral (standardised oil capsules) forms both demonstrate efficacy. It pairs naturally with [[Chamomile]] for daytime anxiety and with [[Valerian]] for sleep-onset anxiety.

### Sleep

Aromatherapy with lavender improves subjective sleep quality, particularly in shift workers, postpartum women, and older adults. Dried flowers in a sachet near the pillow or a few drops on the pillowcase are traditional approaches.

### Headache and Pain

Topical lavender oil, diluted in a carrier, applied to the temples reduces tension headache intensity. Inhalation during migraine onset shows modest benefit. [[Peppermint]] oil is a close comparator for topical headache use.

### Skin

Lavender accelerates wound healing, reduces scarring, and has antifungal activity. It is one of the few essential oils considered safe to apply neat (undiluted) in small amounts to intact skin. See [[Salves and Poultices]].

### Respiratory

Steam inhalation with lavender supports upper respiratory tract infections, though [[Echinacea]] and [[Elderberry]] are better primary herbs for immune support.

## Preparation

| Method | Use Case | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| [[Teas and Infusions\|Tea]] | Anxiety, sleep, digestion | 1 tsp dried flowers per cup, steep 10 min |
| [[Tinctures\|Tincture]] | Daily anxiolytic use | 2–3 ml, 2–3× daily |
| Essential oil (topical) | Skin, pain, headache | Dilute 2–3% in carrier oil; neat only on small areas |
| Essential oil (inhalation) | Anxiety, sleep | Diffuser, sachet, or 2 drops on pillowcase |
| [[Salves and Poultices\|Salve]] | Wound healing, burns | Combine with [[Chamomile]] infusion base |

## Cautions

- Internal use of essential oil: only standardised oral preparations (e.g. Silexan); do not ingest raw essential oil
- Lavender has mild hormonal activity (estrogenic/antiandrogenic at high doses) — avoid prolonged high-dose use in prepubertal children
- Avoid therapeutic internal doses in first trimester pregnancy
