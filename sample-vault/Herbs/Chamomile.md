# Chamomile

Chamomile is one of the oldest and most widely used medicinal herbs in Western herbalism. Its small daisy-like flowers carry a sweet, apple-like scent and a broad range of therapeutic applications, from calming anxious minds to soothing upset stomachs.

## Properties

Chamomile contains several active constituents including apigenin (a flavonoid that binds to GABA receptors), bisabolol, and chamazulene (the compound responsible for its blue colour in essential oil form). Together these produce:

- **Nervine** — calms the nervous system, reduces anxiety and irritability
- **Antispasmodic** — relaxes smooth muscle in the digestive tract and uterus
- **Anti-inflammatory** — bisabolol and chamazulene reduce inflammation topically and internally
- **Carminative** — relieves gas and bloating
- **Mild sedative** — promotes sleep onset without causing morning grogginess

## Medicinal Uses

### Anxiety and Stress

Chamomile is a gentle first-line herb for mild-to-moderate anxiety. Clinical trials have shown it reduces GAD (generalised anxiety disorder) scores over 8–12 weeks. It pairs well with [[Lavender]] and [[Valerian]] for more pronounced calming effects.

### Digestive Complaints

Particularly effective for stress-related digestive upset: IBS, cramping, bloating, and nausea. [[Ginger]] and [[Peppermint]] are complementary herbs for digestive formulas.

### Sleep

A cup of strong chamomile tea 30–45 minutes before bed is a classic remedy for mild insomnia and restless sleep. For more significant sleep disturbances, combine with [[Valerian]].

### Skin and Wound Healing

Topical chamomile — in the form of a poultice, cream, or diluted essential oil — speeds wound healing, reduces eczema flares, and soothes minor burns. See [[Salves and Poultices]].

### Children

One of the few herbs considered safe for infants and children (in appropriate doses). Traditionally used for colic, teething irritability, and fever.

## Preparation

| Method | Use Case | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| [[Teas and Infusions\|Tea]] | Anxiety, sleep, digestion | 1–2 tsp dried flowers per cup, steep 10–15 min covered |
| [[Tinctures\|Tincture]] | Concentrated daily use | 2–4 ml, 3× daily |
| [[Salves and Poultices\|Poultice]] | Skin inflammation | Fresh or rehydrated flowers applied directly |
| Bath | Generalised relaxation | Handful of flowers in muslin bag under running water |

## Cautions

- Avoid therapeutic doses during pregnancy (emmenagogue in very high doses)
- Members of the Asteraceae family with ragweed allergy may react
- May potentiate warfarin — use cautiously with anticoagulants
- Generally well tolerated; one of the safest herbs in common use
