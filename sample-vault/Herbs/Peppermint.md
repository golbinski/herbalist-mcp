# Peppermint

Peppermint is a hybrid of watermint and spearmint and one of the most extensively used aromatic herbs globally — both as a flavouring and as medicine. Its high menthol content gives it a cooling, analgesic character that makes it useful for a strikingly broad range of complaints.

## Properties

Menthol (the dominant constituent at 40–55% of essential oil) activates TRPM8 cold receptors, producing cooling sensations and analgesia. Additional constituents include menthone, menthyl acetate, and rosmarinic acid.

- **Carminative and antispasmodic** — relaxes the lower oesophageal sphincter and intestinal smooth muscle
- **Analgesic** — topical menthol relieves headache, muscle, and joint pain
- **Antimicrobial** — active against H. pylori, Candida, and many respiratory pathogens
- **Decongestant** — menthol's action on cold receptors creates subjective nasal decongestion
- **Mild stimulant** — counteracts [[Valerian]] and [[Chamomile]]-type sedation

## Medicinal Uses

### Irritable Bowel Syndrome

Enteric-coated peppermint oil capsules (which bypass the stomach and release in the small intestine) are one of the best-evidenced herbal treatments for IBS — reducing pain, bloating, and spasm. [[Ginger]] and [[Chamomile]] complement this action for broader digestive formulas.

### Tension Headache

10% peppermint essential oil in ethanol solution applied to the forehead and temples is equivalent to 1000 mg paracetamol in clinical trials for tension headache. [[Lavender]] oil is a useful partner here. See [[Salves and Poultices]] for a simple topical preparation.

### Nausea

Inhalation of peppermint oil is effective for post-operative nausea. [[Ginger]] is generally more effective for motion sickness and morning sickness.

### Respiratory

Menthol inhalation (steam or topical chest rub) subjectively relieves nasal congestion in colds. Evidence for objective decongestion is weak, but the symptomatic relief is valuable.

### Oral Health

Peppermint's antimicrobial activity supports dental hygiene — peppermint extract reduces oral bacteria associated with plaque and bad breath.

## Preparation

| Method | Use Case | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| [[Teas and Infusions\|Tea]] | Digestion, nausea, mild headache | 1–2 tsp fresh or dried leaves, steep 10 min covered |
| Enteric-coated capsule | IBS | 0.2–0.4 ml oil per capsule, 3× daily between meals |
| Essential oil (topical) | Headache, muscle pain | Dilute to 5–10% in carrier; avoid face of infants |
| [[Tinctures\|Tincture]] | Digestive antispasmodic | 2–3 ml before meals |
| Steam inhalation | Respiratory congestion | 3–5 drops in hot water, inhale for 5–10 min |

## Cautions

- **GERD and hiatus hernia**: peppermint relaxes the lower oesophageal sphincter and can worsen acid reflux — use enteric-coated capsules only, or avoid
- **Gallstones**: may trigger biliary colic in susceptible individuals
- **Infants**: never apply peppermint or menthol preparations to the face or chest of children under 2; can cause respiratory arrest
- **Drug interactions**: may inhibit CYP3A4 and CYP1A2 — caution with drugs metabolised by these enzymes
