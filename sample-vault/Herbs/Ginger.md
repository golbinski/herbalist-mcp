# Ginger

Ginger is one of the most universally used medicinal plants across Asian, Middle Eastern, and Western herbal traditions. It is simultaneously a warming digestive, anti-inflammatory, antiemetic, and circulatory stimulant — making it one of the most versatile herbs in the materia medica.

## Properties

Fresh and dried ginger have different phytochemical profiles. Fresh ginger is rich in gingerols (pungent, anti-inflammatory, antiemetic). Upon drying, gingerols convert to shogaols, which are more potent anti-inflammatory and anticancer compounds. Dried ginger is considered "hotter" and more stimulating in Ayurvedic tradition.

- **Antiemetic** — gingerols interact with 5-HT3 receptors and slow gastric emptying; strong evidence for nausea
- **Anti-inflammatory** — inhibits COX-1, COX-2, LOX, and prostaglandin synthesis; synergistic with [[Turmeric]]
- **Carminative** — relieves bloating and gas, stimulates peristalsis
- **Analgesic** — reduces pain in dysmenorrhoea and osteoarthritis
- **Circulatory stimulant** — warming action improves peripheral circulation
- **Antiplatelet** — inhibits thromboxane synthesis (mild blood-thinning effect)

## Medicinal Uses

### Nausea and Vomiting

The best-evidenced herbal antiemetic. Clinical trials support efficacy for:
- Morning sickness (pregnancy nausea) — considered safe at culinary doses
- Chemotherapy-induced nausea (as adjunct)
- Post-operative nausea
- Motion sickness

1–1.5g of dried ginger per day is the typical trial dose. [[Peppermint]] inhalation complements ginger for acute nausea.

### Joint Pain and Arthritis

Comparable to [[Turmeric]] for anti-inflammatory joint applications, and they combine very well. Clinical trials show ginger extract reduces knee OA pain and stiffness. Topical ginger (compress or [[Salves and Poultices|salve]]) adds local warming anti-inflammatory action.

### Dysmenorrhoea

Ginger taken in the first 3 days of menstruation reduces pain comparably to 400 mg ibuprofen in at least one RCT. [[Valerian]] and [[Chamomile]] add antispasmodic support.

### Digestive Stagnation

For "cold" digestive conditions — sluggish motility, bloating after eating, inability to digest fats — ginger's warming carminative action is ideal. Combines with [[Peppermint]] (antispasmodic) and [[Chamomile]] (soothing) for comprehensive digestive formulas.

### Cold and Flu

Hot ginger tea induces perspiration and provides symptomatic relief in early-stage viral illness. Often combined with [[Elderberry]] for antiviral support and honey for demulcent action.

## Preparation

| Method | Use Case | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| [[Teas and Infusions\|Fresh tea]] | Nausea, digestion, colds | 1–2 cm fresh root, sliced, simmered 10–15 min |
| [[Tinctures\|Tincture]] | Anti-inflammatory, joint pain | 3–5 ml, 3× daily |
| Powder in food | Daily anti-inflammatory | ½–1 tsp in smoothies, golden milk with [[Turmeric]] |
| [[Salves and Poultices\|Compress]] | Joint and muscle pain | Strong decoction, applied hot as a compress |
| Capsule | Nausea, joint pain | 250–500 mg dried ginger powder, 2–4× daily |
| Crystallised ginger | Mild nausea, travel | Convenient form; higher sugar content |

## Fresh vs Dried

| | Fresh (gingerols) | Dried (shogaols) |
|--|---|---|
| Flavour | Bright, citrusy, sharp | Hotter, more pungent |
| Antiemetic | Stronger | Moderate |
| Anti-inflammatory | Good | Stronger |
| Circulatory | Warming | Very warming |
| Dosing | 5–10 g fresh | 1–2 g dried |

## Cautions

- **Blood thinners**: high therapeutic doses have antiplatelet activity; caution with warfarin, aspirin, clopidogrel
- **Heartburn/GERD**: warming pungency can aggravate reflux in sensitive individuals
- **Pregnancy**: culinary amounts are safe and beneficial for morning sickness; avoid very high supplemental doses in first trimester
- **Pre-surgery**: as with [[Turmeric]], stop high-dose supplements 2 weeks before surgery
