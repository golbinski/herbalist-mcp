# Elderberry

The elder tree (*Sambucus nigra*) has been called the "medicine chest of the countryside" in European folk tradition. Its dark purple berries and white flowers each have distinct medicinal profiles. Elderberry is now among the most clinically studied herbs for influenza and COVID-19.

## Properties

Ripe cooked elderberries are rich in anthocyanins (particularly cyanidin-3-glucoside and cyanidin-3-sambubioside), quercetin, rutin, and vitamin C. Elderflowers contain flavonoids, mucilage, and volatile oils.

- **Antiviral** — anthocyanins bind to and neutralise influenza virions; inhibit viral attachment to host cells
- **Immunostimulant** — increases cytokine production (TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8) at moderate concentrations
- **Anti-inflammatory** — quercetin inhibits NF-κB and COX-2 pathways
- **Diaphoretic (flowers)** — elderflower promotes sweating, useful in the early stages of febrile illness

## Medicinal Uses

### Influenza and Colds

The strongest evidence base for elderberry. A 2016 randomised trial found elderberry syrup reduced cold duration by an average of 4 days in air travellers. A 2019 meta-analysis concluded elderberry substantially reduced upper respiratory symptoms.

[[Echinacea]] is the natural partner herb: echinacea for immune stimulation, elderberry for direct antiviral activity.

### Fever Management (Elderflower)

Elderflower tea as a diaphoretic is a traditional European fever remedy — particularly in childhood fevers. It promotes sweating in the first stage of fever rather than suppressing it.

### Antioxidant and Cardiovascular

Regular consumption of elderberry (as food-level supplement) is associated with improved antioxidant status and modest improvements in cardiovascular markers.

## Preparation

| Method | Use Case | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| Syrup | Antiviral, immune | Simmer 1 cup berries in 2 cups water 45 min, strain, add honey |
| [[Tinctures\|Tincture]] | Concentrated antiviral | Berry tincture: 4–5 ml, 3–4× daily at acute onset |
| [[Teas and Infusions\|Elderflower tea]] | Fever, sinusitis, diaphoretic | 2 tsp dried flowers per cup, steep 10–15 min |
| Capsule | Convenience | 300–600 mg standardised extract daily |
| Oxymel | Antiviral tonic | Berries extracted in honey-vinegar base; see [[Tinctures]] |

### Syrup Recipe

The classic preparation — simmer dried or fresh elderberries with water, ginger, cinnamon, and cloves, reduce to a syrup, strain, and stir in raw honey (do not boil the honey). Keeps refrigerated for 2–3 months.

## Cautions

- **Raw berries are toxic** — sambunigrin causes nausea and vomiting; always cook before use. Commercially prepared syrups and extracts are safe.
- **Raw bark, leaves, unripe berries, and roots** contain higher concentrations and should not be consumed
- **Autoimmune conditions**: like [[Echinacea]], elderberry stimulates the immune system — theoretical risk of flare in autoimmune conditions
- **Cytokine storm concern**: theoretical (not demonstrated in clinical use) — some practitioners advise reducing dose during active serious viral infection
