# Nervines

Nervines are herbs that act primarily on the nervous system. The term covers a broad spectrum — from gentle relaxants safe for daily use in children, to stronger sedatives requiring careful dosing. Western herbalists traditionally divide nervines into three overlapping groups.

## Types of Nervines

### Nervine Relaxants

Calm and reduce nervous tension without causing significant sedation. Suitable for daily use, high-stress periods, and mild anxiety.

- [[Chamomile]] — the gentlest; safe for infants and the elderly
- [[Lavender]] — aromatic relaxant with good clinical evidence for anxiety

### Nervine Sedatives

More pronounced calming and sleep-promoting effects. Better suited for acute insomnia and significant anxiety rather than daily background use.

- [[Valerian]] — strongest sedative in common use; accumulates over 2–4 weeks

### Nervine Tonics

Herbs that strengthen and restore nervous system function over time, rather than just dampening activity. Often used in recovery from burnout, chronic stress, or prolonged illness.

- [[Lavender]] has mild tonic properties alongside its relaxant action.

## Selecting a Nervine

| Situation | First choice | Add if needed |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| Mild daily anxiety | [[Chamomile]] or [[Lavender]] tea | — |
| Anxiety with sleep problems | [[Lavender]] | [[Valerian]] |
| Persistent insomnia | [[Valerian]] | [[Chamomile]] + [[Lavender]] |
| Acute stress, exam nerves | [[Lavender]] tincture or capsule | [[Chamomile]] |
| Children's anxiety/colic | [[Chamomile]] | — |

## Nervines and the Gut-Brain Axis

A recurring theme in nervines is their dual action on the nervous system and digestive tract. [[Chamomile]] and [[Lavender]] both calm intestinal spasm as well as mental tension — reflecting the enteric nervous system's connection to mood and stress response. This makes nervines particularly appropriate for people whose anxiety manifests in the gut.

## Preparation Principles

Nervines are generally well extracted as teas and tinctures:
- [[Teas and Infusions]] — ideal for daily relaxing use; ritual of preparation is itself calming
- [[Tinctures]] — better for standardised dosing or when rapid onset matters

Avoid combining multiple strong sedative nervines without guidance — especially [[Valerian]] combinations with pharmaceutical sedatives or alcohol.
