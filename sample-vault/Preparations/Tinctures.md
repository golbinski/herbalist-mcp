# Tinctures

A tincture is a concentrated herbal extract made by macerating plant material in alcohol (and sometimes water). Tinctures have a shelf life of 2–5 years, allow precise dosing, and extract a wider range of compounds than water alone — including resins, alkaloids, and many volatile oils that are poorly water-soluble.

## Why Alcohol?

Ethanol is a selective solvent that extracts:
- Most alkaloids
- Resins and oleoresins
- Essential oils (partially)
- Glycosides
- Tannins (partially)
- Some polysaccharides (in water-alcohol mixtures)

Higher alcohol concentrations favour resins and volatile oils; lower concentrations favour glycosides and polysaccharides. Most herbal tinctures use 40–60% ethanol for a balanced extraction.

## Alcohol Percentages by Herb

| Herb | Ideal % | Rationale |
|------|---------|-----------|
| [[Chamomile]] | 40–45% | Primarily water-soluble flavonoids; some volatile oil |
| [[Lavender]] | 45–60% | Volatile oils need higher alcohol |
| [[Valerian]] | 60–70% | Valerenic acid and valepotriates best extracted in higher alcohol |
| [[Echinacea]] root | 60% | Alkamides need higher alcohol; polysaccharides need lower |
| [[Echinacea]] aerial | 40–45% | More polysaccharide-rich; lower alcohol better |
| [[Elderberry]] | 25–40% | Anthocyanins well extracted at lower alcohol; water needed |
| [[Ginger]] | 60–70% | Gingerols and shogaols are lipophilic |
| [[Turmeric]] | 60–70% | Curcuminoids lipophilic; may precipitate on dilution |
| [[Peppermint]] | 45–60% | Menthol and volatile oils |

## Folk Method (Simple Maceration)

Suitable for home preparation with no specialist equipment.

1. Fill a clean jar loosely with chopped or dried herb
2. Cover completely with alcohol (vodka at 40% for soft herbs; higher proof for roots and resins)
3. Cap tightly and store in a cool, dark place
4. Shake daily for 4–6 weeks
5. Strain through muslin or cheesecloth, pressing the marc
6. Bottle in dark glass; label with herb, date, and alcohol used

## Weight-to-Volume (Pharmacy) Method

More precise; used for commercial and professional preparations.

- **Fresh herb**: typically 1:2 (1 part herb by weight, 2 parts menstruum by volume)
- **Dried herb**: typically 1:5 (1 part dried herb by weight, 5 parts menstruum)

Example: 100 g dried [[Valerian]] root + 500 ml of 60% ethanol → 1:5 tincture.

## Dosing

Standard adult doses for most tinctures at 1:5 concentration:

| Herb | Dose | Frequency |
|------|------|-----------|
| [[Chamomile]] | 2–4 ml | 3× daily |
| [[Lavender]] | 2–3 ml | 2–3× daily |
| [[Valerian]] | 4–6 ml | Before bed, or 3× daily |
| [[Echinacea]] | 4–5 ml | 3–5× daily (acute onset) |
| [[Elderberry]] | 4–5 ml | 3–4× daily (acute) |
| [[Ginger]] | 3–5 ml | 3× daily |
| [[Peppermint]] | 2–3 ml | Before meals |
| [[Turmeric]] | 2–4 ml | 3× daily |

Doses should be taken in a small amount of water.

## Special Preparations

### Oxymel

A traditional preparation using apple cider vinegar and honey as menstruum instead of alcohol. Useful for alcohol-sensitive individuals or children. Particularly suited to [[Elderberry]] and [[Ginger]].

### Glycerite

Glycerol (vegetable glycerine) as menstruum. Alcohol-free, sweet tasting, suitable for children. Less efficient extraction than alcohol for many compounds.

### Vinegar Tincture

Apple cider vinegar extracts. Best for alkaloid-containing herbs and mineral-rich plants. Shorter shelf life (1 year).

## Shelf Life and Storage

- Properly made alcohol tinctures: 3–5 years at room temperature in dark glass
- Glycerites: 1–2 years
- Oxymels: 1–2 years refrigerated

## Advantages and Limitations

**Advantages**: long shelf life; concentrated and precise dosing; rapid absorption under the tongue; extracts compounds missed by water; portable.

**Limitations**: alcohol content (though doses are small — typically 0.5–1 ml pure alcohol per dose); more expensive than tea if purchased; takes weeks to make at home; not suitable for alcohol-sensitive individuals.
