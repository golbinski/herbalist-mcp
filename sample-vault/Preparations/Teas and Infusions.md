# Teas and Infusions

The simplest and most accessible form of herbal medicine. A cup of [[Chamomile]] tea before bed or [[Ginger]] tea for a sour stomach is herbal medicine practised by most people without thinking of it as such.

## Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Infusion** | Herb steeped in hot (but not boiling) water; suited to delicate aerial parts — leaves, flowers, soft stems |
| **Decoction** | Herb simmered in water for 15–30 minutes; suited to hard materials — roots, bark, seeds, berries |
| **Cold infusion** | Herb steeped in cold water for several hours; preserves heat-sensitive mucilaginous compounds |
| **Sun tea** | Cold infusion left in sunlight; gentle, slow extraction |

## Basic Infusion Method

1. Measure 1–2 teaspoons of dried herb (or 2–3 teaspoons fresh) per 250 ml water
2. Bring water to appropriate temperature (see below)
3. Pour over herb and cover — the lid traps volatile aromatic compounds
4. Steep for the recommended time
5. Strain and drink

### Temperature Guide

| Herb type | Water temperature | Rationale |
|-----------|------------------|-----------|
| Flowers, leaves (most) | 85–90°C (just off boil) | Prevents bitterness; preserves volatiles |
| Roots and bark | Boiling or simmer | Harder material needs more heat |
| Green and white tea (not medicinal) | 70–80°C | Prevents astringency |

## Decoction Method

For roots, bark, seeds, and dried berries — materials too dense for a simple infusion.

1. Use 1 tablespoon of dried root/bark per 500 ml water
2. Place in a small saucepan with cold water
3. Bring to a gentle boil, then reduce to a simmer
4. Cover and simmer 20–30 minutes
5. Strain, pressing the herb material

Appropriate for: [[Valerian]] root, [[Ginger]] root, [[Elderberry]] berries, [[Turmeric]] fresh rhizome.

## Herb-Specific Notes

| Herb | Method | Temp | Time | Notes |
|------|--------|------|------|-------|
| [[Chamomile]] | Infusion | 85°C | 10–15 min | Cover to retain volatiles |
| [[Lavender]] | Infusion | 85°C | 10 min | Use sparingly — strong flavour |
| [[Valerian]] | Decoction | Simmer | 20 min | Unpleasant smell; tincture preferred |
| [[Peppermint]] | Infusion | 85°C | 10 min | Cover well; very volatile |
| [[Ginger]] | Decoction | Boil | 15 min | Add honey and lemon to taste |
| [[Elderberry]] | Decoction | Simmer | 30–45 min | For syrup base |
| [[Elderberry\|Elderflower]] | Infusion | 85°C | 10–15 min | Light and pleasant |
| [[Turmeric]] | Decoction | Simmer | 15 min | Add black pepper and fat |
| [[Echinacea]] | Decoction (root) | Simmer | 20 min | Or infusion of aerial parts |

## Proportions for Therapeutic Use

General medicinal doses are higher than "wellness" tea proportions:

- Standard: 1–2 tsp dried herb per cup
- Medicinal: 2–4 tsp dried herb per cup, or use a smaller volume of water
- Strong decoction: 2 tbsp root per 500 ml, reduced to 250 ml

## Storage

- Freshly brewed tea: drink within a few hours; can refrigerate for up to 24 hours
- Herb material: store dried herbs in airtight containers away from light and heat; most remain potent for 1–2 years

## Advantages and Limitations

**Advantages**: cheapest preparation; easy to make at home; fast onset; water extracts many polysaccharides and glycosides effectively; ritual of preparation has psychological benefit.

**Limitations**: volatile compounds lost if uncovered; poor extraction of resins and some alkaloids; short shelf life; inconvenient for travel; dosing less precise than tinctures.

For [[Valerian]] in particular, [[Tinctures]] are strongly preferred due to poor palatability and better alcohol extraction of valerenic acid.
