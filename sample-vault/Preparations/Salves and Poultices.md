# Salves and Poultices

Topical preparations bring herbal medicine directly to the site of action — bypassing digestion entirely for local skin, muscle, and joint complaints. They are among the oldest forms of herbal medicine; evidence of herbal salves has been found in archaeological sites dating back thousands of years.

## Poultices

The simplest topical preparation — herb material applied directly to the skin.

### Fresh Poultice

1. Bruise or crush fresh plant material (leaves, flowers, or root) in a mortar
2. Apply directly to the affected area
3. Cover with a clean cloth and secure with bandage or tape
4. Leave 20–60 minutes; renew as needed

Best herbs for fresh poultices:
- [[Chamomile]] flowers — inflammation, irritated skin, slow-healing wounds
- [[Lavender]] flowers — minor burns, insect bites, infected wounds
- [[Ginger]] root (grated) — joint pain, muscle aches (warming)

### Dried Herb Poultice

1. Rehydrate dried herb in a small amount of boiling water to form a paste
2. Allow to cool to comfortable temperature
3. Apply as above

### Ginger Compress

A classic warming anti-inflammatory treatment for joint and muscle pain:

1. Make a strong [[Ginger]] decoction (4 tbsp dried ginger per litre, simmered 20 min)
2. Soak a cloth in the hot liquid (as hot as comfortably tolerable)
3. Wring out and apply to the affected joint or muscle
4. Cover with a dry cloth to retain heat
5. Re-soak and reapply every 5 minutes for 20–30 minutes

## Herbal Oils (Infused Oils)

The base for most salves. Infused oils extract fat-soluble compounds from herbs.

### Cold Infusion (Folk Method)

1. Fill a jar with dried herb (not fresh — water in fresh herb can cause rancidity)
2. Cover completely with a carrier oil (olive, sunflower, or sweet almond oil)
3. Seal and leave in a warm place for 4–6 weeks, shaking daily
4. Strain and bottle in dark glass

Best for: [[Chamomile]], [[Lavender]], [[Calendula]] (not in this vault but excellent for skin)

### Hot Infusion (Quick Method)

1. Place herb and oil in a double boiler or slow cooker on lowest setting
2. Maintain at 40–60°C (too hot destroys volatile compounds) for 4–8 hours
3. Strain and bottle

## Salves

A salve (or balm) is an infused oil stiffened with beeswax to produce a semi-solid consistency.

### Basic Salve Recipe

**Ingredients:**
- 100 ml infused herbal oil (or combination)
- 10–15 g beeswax (adjust for desired firmness: more wax = firmer)

**Method:**
1. Melt beeswax in a double boiler
2. Remove from heat; stir in infused oil
3. Test consistency by dropping a small amount on a cold plate — adjust wax as needed
4. Pour immediately into tins or small glass jars before it sets
5. Label with herb(s), date, and intended use

### Salve Combinations

| Combination | Primary use |
|-------------|-------------|
| [[Chamomile]] + [[Lavender]] | General wound healing, eczema, minor burns |
| [[Lavender]] + [[Peppermint]] | Headache temples, muscle tension |
| [[Ginger]] + [[Turmeric]] | Arthritis, deep muscle aches |
| [[Echinacea]] + [[Chamomile]] | Infected or slow-healing skin |

## Essential Oil Additions

A small amount of essential oil can be added to a finished salve for extra potency or fragrance. Add after removing from heat (to prevent evaporation):

- [[Lavender]]: 1–2% of total weight — skin healing, antimicrobial
- [[Peppermint]]: 1–2% — cooling, analgesic, headache
- [[Chamomile]] (German, blue): 0.5–1% — strongly anti-inflammatory

## Shelf Life and Storage

- Infused oils: 6–12 months in a dark, cool location; refrigerate to extend
- Salves: 12–24 months at room temperature; signs of rancidity are off smell or colour change
- Add a few drops of vitamin E oil as a natural preservative

## Advantages and Limitations

**Advantages**: direct local action; avoids systemic side effects and drug interactions; pleasant to use; relatively easy to make; long shelf life for salves.

**Limitations**: not suitable for open wounds or broken skin with some preparations; limited penetration depth; correct preparation matters for efficacy.
